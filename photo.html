Pelican Nebula

📅 July 7th, 2022

📍 Montecito, CA


An emission nebula located in the Cygnus constellation. This wider angle image allows you to see some of the surrounding nebulosity, as well as some of the North American nebula (towards the top).

For this image, I spent time getting the guide scope calibrated so I could actually use it. This smaller scope which sits on top of the main optical tube helps keep the mount tracking correctly by sending small pulses to the main mount to correct for any slight drift as it takes each long exposure.

M81, M82

📅 April 3rd, 2022

📍 Lake Arrowhead State Park, TX


Spring time is known as Galaxy season because many galaxies are out and about in the night sky. M81, also known as Bode’s Galaxy, is the larger of these two galaxies, located on the right in this image. The Cigar Galaxy is the smudge of a neighbor to the left. This pair of galaxies are both over 11 million light years away.

First time the new EQR6 mount came with many difficulties and learning moments, but I am happy with this result (and honestly just being able to get time to shoot with cooperating weather).

Lunar Eclipse

📅 November 20th, 2021

📍 Lake Brownwood State Park, TX


What happens when the sun, earth and moon all line up? An eclipse, and in this case, a lunar one. The earths shadow is being cast onto the moon, and over the course of a few hours a very bright white full moon turns silver, then gray, then a dark red color.

Why the red color you ask? This happens because of a phenomenon called “Rayleigh scattering”, where different wavelengths of light are refracted in different ways. In this case the red wavelength (longer) light is scattered less, and therefore we see it reflected back to us on the surface of the moon during an eclipse.

This single 1second exposure shows maximum coverage of this particular lunar eclipse. The white sliver on the left is the tiny percentage of the moon that wasn’t covered by earths shadow.

Orion Nebula v1

📅 November 18th, 2021

📍 Lake Brownwood State Park, TX


My first ever deep sky object, and one of my personal favorites, the Great Orion Nebula.

This nebula is about 1300 light years away, meaning the light captured in this image is 1300 years old. To equate that to our earthly timeline, this light seen here was forming sometime in the 7th century, the beginning of the Dark Ages in Europe…
